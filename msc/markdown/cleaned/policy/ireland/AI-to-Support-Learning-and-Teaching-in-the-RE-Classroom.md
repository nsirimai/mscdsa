## Children, young people and parents' views on AI at school

This figure presents three survey findings about children, young people, and parents' awareness and concerns regarding AI.

The key findings are:

- 54% of children and young people want to learn more about AI at school.
- 82% of children and young people said their parents or teachers know “some”, “not too much”, or “nothing” about AI.
- 93% of parents surveyed said they have concerns about their children using AI.

The figure is relevant because it shows a gap between young people's interest in AI education and the perceived lack of adult knowledge or confidence around AI. It also highlights strong parental concern about children's AI use.

## Parents, teachers and children reporting AI knowledge, training and uncertainty

This figure presents three survey findings about parents, teachers, and children's relationship to AI learning and training.

The key findings are:

- 87% of parents felt their children learning about AI now was either “important” or “very important”.
- 95% of teachers have not received any training in AI.
- 52% of children “don't know” if AI will make the future better or not.

The figure is relevant because it shows a strong perceived need for AI education, while also revealing a major teacher-training gap. It also indicates uncertainty among children about the future impact of AI.

## Parents' conversations and concerns about children using AI

This figure presents three survey findings about parental conversations, concerns, and children's reported help with AI.

The key findings are:

- 59% of parents said that the topic of AI comes up in conversation with their children either “never” or “not much”.
- 93% of parents reported having “some” or “a lot” of concerns about their children using AI.
- 57% of children said the help they received with AI from their parents or teachers had been “none” or “not too much”.

The figure is relevant because it shows a policy-practice and support gap: parents are concerned about AI, but AI is not frequently discussed with children, and many children report receiving limited help from adults.
