# Press release on AI literacy, privacy and trust

At its core, the ethical use of AI depends on the protection of the human dignity and agency of children. However, with pedagogical decisions increasingly being delegated to algorithms, there is a significant risk that children are reduced to mere data points, undermined by systems that lack the human empathy and intuition essential for responding to their emotional and psychological needs. This algorithmic mediation of learning can further erode a child's autonomy and their right to exercise individual choice. The deployment of AI systems in education also threatens a child's right to be protected from discrimination, as biased data and historical stereotypes often perpetuate existing inequalities. And, finally, the invasive nature of AIED systems, which continuously monitor student behaviour and emotions, also creates a panoptic effect that compromises the right to privacy. Perhaps most critically, AIED systems also facilitate the commercial exploitation of children's data. By extracting learner interactions to train proprietary models for profit, commercial developers in effect treat children as commodities and unpaid labour.

Funded by the European Commission, the Artificial Intelligence for and by Teachers (AIT) project was a three-year experiment to explore and support the use of AI in education. It consisted of producing, implementing and evaluating professional learning activities with the goal of acculturating teachers to AI. The project was conducted in five countries: France, Slovenia, Italy, Ireland and Luxemburg. Seventeen partners, including education ministries, evaluators and research labs took part in the project, under the coordination of France Education Internationale (FEI).

Much of the talk around AI in schools has understandably centred on students' use of it and the ways in which this might undermine the academic integrity of State exams. This is a serious issue, with many of the concerns of teachers and their unions being essentially shrugged off. Given the potential for students to use AI tools to assist with their AACs, the State Examinations Commission (SEC) has, belatedly, issued guidance to students and their teachers on the use of AI for these projects. The Coursework Rules & Procedures - is an attempt to clarify the permissible and non-permissible use of AI. What these rules are, in essence, is an acknowledgment that AI exists, that students will use it, and tasks teachers with ensuring that they are using it minimally.
