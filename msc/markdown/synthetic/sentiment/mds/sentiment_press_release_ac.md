The EC's updated guidelines, Ethical Guidelines on the Use of AI and Data in Teaching and Learning for Educators (), offers a framework for the ethical and responsible integration of AI and data in educational settings, primarily targeting teachers and school leaders. The document covers three key areas: the core principles and legal context (including the EU AI Act and GDPR), guiding questions and practical scenarios for classroom application, and a suite of background resources and technical definitions. Key findings and recommendations emphasise that AI should remain humancentred (although what that means is never elaborated), serving as a tool to enhance rather than replace the teacher's role (which is not what most AIED tools do), while addressing generic AI risks such as algorithmic bias and data privacy, as well as the potential for over-reliance. The document also highlights five deontological ethical considerations (human dignity, fairness, trustworthiness, academic integrity, and justified choice) that it argues are essential for ensuring that AI technologies respect human rights and promote the best interests of all students (although human rights implications and the impact on disadvantaged students are rarely addressed by AIED developers). Unfortunately, however, the document also includes some unhelpful anthropomorphisms (such as using the inaccurate word 'hallucination' to mean the nonsense or confabulations that are frequently generated by GenAI). Anthropomorphising AI misleads users into over-estimating the capabilities and empathy of AI systems, and it could be argued has contributed to young people around the world committing suicide (Chatterjee, ) or dying by other means (e.g., drug overdoses, Black & Council, ).
