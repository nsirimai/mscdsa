In contemporary ethical discourse, virtue ethics is often acknowledged as a foundational tradition but is rarely drawn on in practice (it is challenging to codify character or write a law that mandates a person to be wise). Instead, contemporary frameworks tend to rely on a pragmatic blending of deontology and consequentialism (D/C). This occurs as institutions attempt to protect universal human rights and duties, such as privacy and dignity, while simultaneously striving to achieve the 'best overall result' for the greatest number of people. Consequently, ethical guidelines often function as a hybrid system where fixed moral rules are used to set boundaries, but the specific actions within those boundaries are justified by their perceived social utility and outcomes.
