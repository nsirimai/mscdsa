Over the last months we have had a number of schools and trusts ask us about AI policies, so we thought we might be able to help with a few pointers that you may wish to consider when developing or reviewing an AI policy. It reflects current regulatory, safeguarding and assessment expectations and is intended to support safe, responsible and transparent use of AI, while maintaining clear accountability and oversight.

These notes are shared primarily for schools and trusts in England. Some legislation and principles apply UK-wide; where relevant, references to Wales and Scotland are included for context.

An AI policy will usually apply to staff, pupils, governors, trustees, volunteers and contractors, though you may wish to adjust it to reflect local context.

## 1. Purpose of the policy

Schools and trusts may wish to set out:

- Why an AI policy is needed
- What it aims to achieve
- How it supports safeguarding, accountability and compliance

## Example wording schools may adapt:

This policy sets out how artificial intelligence (AI) tools may be used across the school/trust. It is intended to ensure AI is used safely, responsibly and transparently, while maintaining clear accountability for decisions, safeguarding pupils, and meeting statutory and regulatory requirements.

Clarifying purpose helps ensure AI use aligns with existing governance and statutory responsibilities rather than developing informally.



## 2. Scope and definition

An AI policy should clearly define what is meant by 'AI' in a school context. This may include, but is not limited to:

- Text or image generation tools
- Assessment or feedback support tools
- Data analysis or prediction tools
- Safeguarding, behaviour or monitoring systems using automated decision-making

## Example wording:

For the purposes of this policy, AI refers to digital systems or tools that perform tasks normally requiring human judgement.

It should also be clear that AI tools may support professional activity, but do not replace professional judgement.

## 3. Principles for AI use

Many schools choose to establish a small set of guiding principles to underpin AI use. These often include considerations such as:

- Human oversight: AI outputs should always be reviewed by an appropriate member of staff
- Accountability: Responsibility for decisions supported by AI remains with the school or trust
- Safeguarding first: AI use must not compromise pupil safety or wellbeing
- Data protection: Personal data must be handled in line with UK GDPR and data protection legislation
- Transparency: Staff and pupils should understand when AI is being used and for what purpose
- Fairness: AI should not be used in ways that unfairly disadvantage individuals or groups

## Example wording:

AI outputs must always be reviewed by an appropriate member of staff. Responsibility for decisions supported by AI remains with the school/trust.

These principles help provide consistency across different use cases.



## 4. Acceptable and unacceptable use

An effective policy usually sets out:

- Where AI use is considered appropriate (for example, supporting planning or administrative tasks)
- Clear boundaries on what AI must not be used for

## Example wording:

AI tools may be used where they support teaching, learning or administrative activity, provided appropriate professional oversight is maintained.

Schools and trusts may wish to make explicit that AI should not:

- Make final decisions about pupils without human review
- Process pupil data in unapproved systems
- Replace professional judgement in safeguarding, SEND or high-stakes assessment decisions
- Be used as the sole basis for exclusions, SEND identification or pupil risk scoring
- Breach examination regulations or academic integrity requirements.

## 5. Use in teaching, learning and assessment

Policies often clarify how AI can support teaching and learning while protecting the integrity of assessment.

## Example wording:

Staff may use AI tools to support planning, resource development and administrative tasks related to teaching and learning.

Schools may also make clear that AI must not:

- Complete assessed work on behalf of pupils
- Generate pupil examination responses
- Undermine the validity of assessment outcomes

AI use should align with current assessment regulations, including JCQ, SQA or equivalent guidance depending on jurisdiction.



## 6. Use by pupils

Pupil use of AI is commonly addressed separately from staff use. Policies may set expectations around:

- Age-appropriate access
- Supervision and staff oversight
- Alignment with curriculum objectives and acceptable use policies

## Example wording:

Pupil use of AI tools must be supervised and aligned with curriculum objectives and acceptable use policies.

Clear guidance helps pupils understand appropriate and inappropriate use, particularly in relation to homework, coursework and assessments.

## 7. Data protection and privacy

Schools and trusts should consider how AI use interacts with data protection responsibilities.

## Example wording:

AI tools that process personal data must be approved by the school/trust and have appropriate data processing agreements in place. The Data Protection Officer (DPO) will be consulted where AI tools involve personal data, profiling, monitoring, or any form of automated decision support. The DPO should be involved at an early stage where tools are pupil-facing, process special category data, or may present higher risk.

## Policies often state that:

- AI tools processing personal data must be approved before use
- The DPO may need to be involved in reviewing DPIAs, contracts or high-risk processing activities
- Appropriate data processing agreements must be in place
- UK GDPR and relevant data protection legislation must be followed



## 7. Data protection and privacy cont.

Policies may also include:

- Clear examples of DPIA triggers, such as the use of pupil data, special category data, profiling or monitoring, automated decision support, third-country processing, or model training on school data
- Plain-English guidance for staff on what information must not be entered into public or unapproved AI tools (for example names, dates of birth, SEN status, safeguarding information, photos or unique identifiers)

Schools may wish to note that changes introduced through the Data Use and Access Act 2025 are being phased in through June 2026, and that data protection practices should be kept under review as this develops.

## 8. Procurement and approval

Many schools and trusts choose to set clear expectations around how AI-enabled tools are introduced. This may include:

- Requiring AI-enabled products to be reviewed and approved before use
- Using compliant procurement routes where appropriate
- Assessing safeguarding, data protection and security risks

## Example wording:

All AI-enabled tools must be reviewed and approved before use and procured through compliant routes where required.

Schools may also wish to require that any generative AI product used with staff or pupils evidences alignment with DfE Generative AI: product safety standards (updated 19 January 2026), including expectations around filtering and monitoring, security, privacy, governance and child development safeguards.

It may be helpful to make explicit that existing safeguarding, filtering and monitoring duties continue to apply where AI changes the risk profile of a system.



## 9. Training and awareness

An AI policy may outline how schools and trusts will:

- Provide staff with practical guidance on appropriate AI use
- Communicate expectations clearly and consistently
- Support AI literacy at leadership level

Staff are usually encouraged to raise questions or concerns where they are unsure.

## Example wording:

Staff are expected to engage with guidance and raise concerns where they are unsure about appropriate AI use.

## 10. Monitoring and review

Given how quickly AI tools evolve, policies often include arrangements for:

- Ongoing oversight by senior leadership
- Reassessment of tools as risks or capabilities change
- Regular policy review

In addition to a scheduled review date, schools may wish to identify review triggers, such as:

- Updates to DfE AI product safety standards or support materials
- Changes to Ofsted's published approach
- Updates to ICO guidance on AI, profiling or automated decision-making
- Changes to examination body guidance (for example JCQ, SQA, WJEC or CCEA)
- Significant changes to supplier functionality or data use

## 11. Links to other policies

AI policies typically sit alongside existing arrangements, such as:

- Safeguarding and Child Protection
- Data Protection
- Acceptable Use
- Assessment
- Behaviour

## 12. Ownership and accountability

Finally, schools and trusts may wish to record:

- Who is responsible for the policy
- When it was approved
- When it will be reviewed

Clear ownership supports accountability and supports effective governance.
