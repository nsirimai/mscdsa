## PATH principles for AI in education

This figure presents the PATH principles as a framework for the responsible use of AI in education. The acronym PATH is represented through four connected arrow-shaped blocks: P, A, T, and H. Each letter corresponds to one principle.

- **P — Promote teaching and learning:** Use AI to enhance teaching and learning when the benefits outweigh the costs, evaluate AI use, and encourage effective thinking and assessment practices.
- **A — Advance wellbeing and social interaction:** Use AI to support the wellbeing and social interaction of both educators and learners.
- **T — Train for AI literacy:** Equip both educators and students with the knowledge and values to understand and use AI ethically and effectively.
- **H — Harness AI ethically:** Align AI use with broad ethical principles of transparency, responsibility and accountability, privacy, justice and fairness, and human, child, and worker rights.

## Transforming learning with AI systems

The rapid popularisation of AI systems signifies a major shift in teaching and learning, bringing both new opportunities and significant challenges. Therefore, as education systems worldwide confront two key imperatives-preparing learners for an increasingly unpredictable and rapidly evolving future and ensuring every student is fully engaged, successful, and ready for the demands of tomorrow (ACER, 2022)-understanding how to maximise the benefits of AI systems while overcoming its challenges becomes a critical priority. To help guide educators, this report:

1. Outlines a set of guiding principles for using AI systems in teaching and learning.
2. Provides practical examples of how AI systems can be used in teaching and learning.

## What is an AI system?

There are numerous definitions of AI systems. Here, we use the following definition:

An AI system is a machine-based system that, for explicit or implicit objectives, infers, from the input it receives, how to generate outputs such as predictions, content, recommendations, or decisions that can influence physical or virtual environments. Different AI systems vary in their levels of autonomy and adaptiveness after deployment. (OECD, 2024, p. 4)

A generative AI system is a type of AI system that is designed to create new content or data, such as text, images, audio, video, code, or other formats, that resembles or mimics the patterns found in its training data. These systems rely on advanced machine learning models, often based on neural networks.

Generative AI systems work by learning the underlying structure and features of their training data during the training phase. Once trained, they can produce original outputs that are not direct replicas of the input data but follow the same style, rules, or patterns. For instance:

- Text Generation . Creating coherent and contextually relevant writing (e.g., ChatGPT; openai.com).
- Image Generation . Producing realistic or stylised images based on descriptions or prompts (e.g., MidJourney; midjourney.com).
- Music Generation. Composing melodies or full tracks (e.g., Amper Music; ampermusic.com).
- Code Generation. Writing or suggesting code snippets for developers (e.g., GitHub Copilot; github.com).
- Video Generation. Creating or editing video content (e.g., Runway ML; runwayml.com).
- Speech and Voice Generation. Synthesising realistic speech or creating custom voiceovers (e.g., Descript Overdub; descript.com).

## Opportunities and challenges of AI systems for teaching and learning

AI systems have the potential to revolutionise education, transforming teaching and learning through innovative applications (Yan et al., 2024). For instance, by automating routine tasks like grading and attendance tracking, AI can enhance efficiency, freeing teachers to focus on more complex and creative aspects of education (Wang et al., 2024). It also may support personalised learning by providing tailored assessments and feedback, thereby improving learning effectiveness by addressing the unique needs of individual students (Wang et al., 2024).

Beyond enhancing existing practices, AI enables the reimagining of learning experiences. Adaptive learning systems, for instance, have the potential to adjust to a learner's knowledge, motivation and emotions, creating a dynamic and responsive educational environment (Wang et al., 2024). Furthermore, AI opens doors to previously unfeasible opportunities, such as immersive VR and AR simulations that may provide learners access to experiences not previously possible (Wang et al., 2024).

However, the integration of AI in teaching and learning also has its challenges (e.g., Adams et al., 2023). Concerns must be carefully addressed about impacts on teaching and learning, wellbeing and social interaction, AI literacy of users, transparency, responsibility and accountability, privacy, justice and fairness, and human, child, and workers' rights. By navigating these challenges thoughtfully, AI can become a transformative tool in fostering positive outcomes in education.

## Overview of the guidance

This document has two main sections. First, we present principles for using AI for teaching and learning. Second, we provide practical examples of how AI can be used for teaching and learning. To introduce the principles, a human-centred approach is put forward as an overarching principle for using AI in teaching and learning (similar to UNESCO, 2021). This is an approach where technology is designed and used in ways that prioritise human interests (Shneiderman, 2022). This human-centred approach is embodied in the PATH principles proposed in this document. These principles aim to help educators navigate the integration of AI into teaching and learning ethically and effectively. The PATH principles are:

1. Promote teaching and learning . Use AI to enhance teaching and learning when the benefits outweigh the costs, evaluate AI use, and encourage effective thinking and assessment practices.
2. Advance wellbeing and social interaction . Use AI to support the wellbeing and social interaction of both educators and learners.
3. Train for AI literacy . Equip both educators and students with the knowledge and values to understand and use AI ethically and effectively.
4. Harness AI Ethically . Align AI use with broad ethical principles of transparency, responsibility and accountability, privacy, justice and fairness, and human, child, and worker rights.

Whilst generated in Australia, the guidance outlined in this document draws upon international evidence and is applicable in a range of international contexts and school settings. In interpreting and applying the contents of this document, readers are encouraged to do so within the relevant legislative and regulatory frameworks within their specific contexts.

## Development of the guidance

The aim of this research was to provide educational professionals with guidance on using AI effectively and ethically for teaching and learning. To this end, we posed the following two research questions:

1. What guiding principles should be considered when using AI for teaching and learning?
2. How can AI meaningfully be used in teaching and learning?

## Development of the PATH principles

To answer the first research question, the initial strategy was to conduct a rapid review. During this process, existing reviews were explored, leading to the identification of a synthesis of AI principles in education as per Adams et al. (2023) (see Figure 1, step 1). Adams et al.'s principles extend the work of Jobin et al.'s (2019) review of 85 ethical frameworks for AI use by synthesising education-specific guidance from The World Economic Forum (2019), The Institute for Ethical AI in Education (IEAIE) (2021), UNESCO (2021), and UNICEF (2021).

Although Adams et al.'s (2023) framework offers guidance for using AI in teaching and learning, it presents several limitations for broader application:

1. The framework focuses on K-12 education, potentially overlooking principles related to tertiary education.
2. The documents reviewed may not have specifically addressed the unique opportunities and challenges posed by generative AI.
3. The analysis does not incorporate more recent guidance, which could offer updated insights.
4. The principles lack coherence, as some principles overlap significantly.
5. The presentation and articulation of the principles could be refined to enhance their clarity and applicability for educators.

Therefore, additional documents were analysed by the first author to evaluate how well Adams et al.'s framework aligns with more recent guidelines, as well as its coherence and usability (Figure 1, Step 2). This resulted in the development of a more comprehensive, up-to-date, coherent, and potentially more practical set of principles (see Figure 1, Step 3).

After reviewing the additional guidance, an analysis was conducted to evaluate its alignment with the principles established by Adams et al. (2023). Overall, there was substantial alignment between Adams et al.'s principles and the more recent guidance. However, the newer guidance offered further elaboration and introduced additional considerations. For example, the newer guidance led to:

- Expanded principles . For instance, Adams et al.'s principle of 'Teacher wellbeing' was broadened to include the wellbeing of students and other stakeholders; the principle on 'Children's Rights' was extended to incorporate 'Human and Worker Rights'.
- New additions and sub-components . For instance, 'Thinking' was added as a sub-component under 'Promote teaching and learning', and 'Academic integrity' was incorporated as a subcomponent of 'Transparency'. Further, the principle of 'Responsibility and accountability' was broken down into the sub-components of 'Oversight and human agency', 'Contestability and remedy', 'Protection, empowerment, and human Support', and 'Evaluation'.

In considering the coherence and usability of Adams et al.'s principles for educators, it was concluded that improvements could be achieved by consolidating overlapping principles and grouping broadly applicable ethical principles under a single overarching principle-'Harness AI Ethically'-for greater clarity. This approach helped distinguish education-specific ethical principles from those applicable outside of education settings while also reducing the overall number of principles to a more manageable and memorable set. The principles of 'Beneficence,' 'Non-maleficence,' and 'Freedom and Autonomy' from Adams et al. were found to overlap with the principles of 'Advancing wellbeing and social interaction' and 'Responsibility and accountability' proposed here. Therefore, these principles were consolidated. Furthermore, to increase engagement and memorability of the principles for educational professionals, the acronym 'PATH' and infographic were created. Clearer and more actionable labels were also introduced to enhance usability. In other words, the principles presented in this document are consistent with those of Adams et al. (2023) but elaborated upon and presented in an arguably more coherent and useable manner for educators. Additionally, the alignment of recent guidelines with these principles ensures greater relevance.

## 1. Promote teaching and learning

Due to the affordances provided by AI systems, their use in teaching and learning should be considered. However, as the guidelines reviewed make clear, AI should not be used haphazardly. Instead of using AI due to hype, it is essential to ensure its use promotes teaching and learning, which has three essential components:

1. Benefit . The use of AI should be considered when the benefits outweigh the costs.
2. Evaluation . The evaluation of AI should be considered to ensure it effectively enhances the educational experience.
3. Thinking. To effectively encourage and evaluate learning, educators should consider designing work that AI cannot complete, encouraging higher-order thinking, creativity, and critical analysis of AI, and providing clear guidance regarding how students can use AI in their work, such as during assessments.

As highlighted in the creation of many of the documents and in some of their guidance, co-creation should be considered when designing and evaluating AI. For instance, in consultation with various stakeholders during the creation of the guidelines reviewed, it was highlighted that while there is much excitement that AI systems may provide more access to personalised learning pathways tailored to each student's unique needs, there are concerns. One major challenge stakeholders discussed was the potential reduction in student-teacher relations (see IEAIED, 2021b), which are vital for learning and engagement (Roorda et al., 2011). Therefore, when deciding to implement an AI system that offers personalised learning pathways, educators should carefully evaluate if the benefit of the system outweighs any costs, such as loss of social interaction. Additionally, it is important to consider strategies for mitigating these costs, regularly assess the AI system's overall impact and fit, and consider co-creating AI solutions and evidence with numerous stakeholders.

Another aspect of prioritising learning is to consider designing work for students that AI cannot complete and encouraging higher-order thinking, creativity, and critical thinking. For instance, educators can use oral presentations or invigilated assessments to assess students' ability to apply and evaluate information and think critically, ensuring the work reflects their own thinking, knowledge, and capabilities. Additionally, educators can use collaborative projects, where students work together to solve complex, open-ended problems that encourage innovation, teamwork, and adaptability.

The second section of this report provides some practical examples of using of AI systems for teaching and learning. Some potential considerations when evaluating AI systems and their use are:

- Effectiveness . Does AI use achieve the intended goal? Are the benefits worth the costs? (i.e., Does the use of AI align with 'Principle 1. Promoting Teaching and Learning'?)
- Efficiency . Does AI use achieve the intended goal in a timely manner?
- Wellbeing and social interaction. What is the impact of AI on student and/or teacher wellbeing and social interaction? (see 'Principle 2. Advance wellbeing and social interaction')
- AI literacy. Do users have appropriate levels of AI literacy, such as how to use the system effectively and ethically? (see 'Principle 3. Train for AI literacy')
- Ethics. Does the use of AI align with broad ethical principles for AI use, such as justice and fairness, privacy, and transparency? (see 'Principle 4. Harness AI ethically')

Code.org et al. (2023, pp. 16-20) lists further benefits, risks, and mitigation strategies when employing AI systems. ## 2. Advance wellbeing and social interaction

When using AI in education, it is important to prioritise the wellbeing of teachers, students, and other stakeholders and foster meaningful social interactions.

At a minimum, it is important to consider if AI is safe and harm-free 1 . Moreover, AI use ideally attempts to enhance-rather than replace-human teaching and crucial human social connections, such as those between students and teachers or among students and their peers. The use of AI should be considered to reduce teachers' workloads and stressors, thereby increasing their capacity to focus on core teaching activities. Additionally, teachers' expertise should be considered, with AI used in a way that respects and augments their human decision-making. Teachers' rights and working conditions are important considerations as well.

Student wellbeing is also paramount. AI tools should aim to support a balanced academic experience. Preserving students' digital and non-digital activities such as play and leisure time, social interactions, and school breaks is important. Rather than increasing pressure around homework and assessments, AI should be considered to help alleviate these stressors. Finally, AI systems focused on enhancing wellbeing should also be considered; for example, systems that can identify when students or teachers may require mental health support can assist in providing targeted support as required.

This principle also intersects with other principles, such as AI literacy, as having knowledge of how to use AI can prevent unnecessary stress and workload for teachers as they navigate AI integration in their classrooms. Ensuring teachers are equipped with the necessary skills to evaluate AI tools fosters a balanced and informed approach to technology adoption in education. Elements of evaluation and co-design discussed in the previous section are relevant here as well; part of evaluating AI is to weigh up the benefits or costs associated with wellbeing . Including teachers and students in the design and feedback processes of AI tools is also helpful for ensuring that these tools align with advancing enduser wellbeing. ## 3. Train for AI literacy

As AI becomes ubiquitous in learning, teaching, and working, it is crucial for both teachers and students to understand what AI is, how it works, how to use it effectively and ethically and hold appropriate values and attitudes around its use (UNESCO, 2024b). These aspectsknown as AI literacy-are essential to foster to prepare students for the future and ensure that they are equipped for the demands of an ever-changing society. Being an AI-literate educator is important for understanding and implementing the other PATH principles.

Fostering AI literacy offers numerous benefits for teaching and learning. For example, for educators, it enhances readiness to make informed and responsible decisions on when and how to teach about AI, helps them teach about AI's ethical and societal implications, and supports their ongoing professional growth (UNESCO, 2024b). For students, AI literacy is important for 'Fostering a critical approach to AI', which 'Prioritizes human-centered interactions with AI systems,' and contributes to 'Encouraging environmentally sustainable AI' (UNESCO, 2024a, pp. 14-15).

## 4. Harness AI ethically

AI systems and their use should be guided by broad ethical principles that extend beyond education. These include transparency in how AI operates and is employed, responsibility and accountability for its outcomes, protecting privacy, upholding justice and fairness, and respect for the rights of humans, children, and workers. Each principle warrants careful consideration and is further outlined below.

## Transparency

Strive to be transparent when integrating AI into teaching and learning. This has three aspects:

- Disclosure . Ensure that the use of AI (and policies surrounding its use) are communicated openly, allowing students, educators, and caregivers to be fully aware of their personal responsibilities, when AI systems are being used, and what role AI systems play in educational settings.
- Understanding . Clarify how AI systems work so that users can accurately assess and judge the outputs. This requires explaining AI processes in ways that are accessible to all, fostering an informed understanding of the limitations of AI output.
- Academic integrity and copyright. Students are supported to attribute AI appropriately, and when AI is used, it should adhere to copyright.

## Responsibility and accountability

## When integrating AI into teaching and learning, consider responsible and accountable practices, adhering to:

- Oversight and human agency . Educators are encouraged to retain decision-making authority and approve AI decisions, using AI as a supportive tool rather than replacing human judgment. This ensures that AI enhances, rather than overrides, the expertise and insight educators offer. It also ensures that educators are responsible and accountable for decisions.
- Contestability and remedy . Education stakeholders ought to have the opportunity to question and challenge AI use and decisions, as well as to seek remedies when appropriate.
- Protection, empowerment, and human support. AI systems should aim to protect and empower users, incorporating safeguards that encourage safe and positive engagement with the digital world. It is recommended that human support be available to provide assistance when needed. Furthermore, it may be worth exploring the possibility of offering human alternatives to AI systems where appropriate.
- Evaluation . As mentioned in other principles, it is important to consider assessing AI before, during, and after its deployment to ensure it effectively serves its intended purpose and that any potential issues are managed appropriately.

## Justice and fairness

When integrating AI into teaching and learning, strive for just and fair practices, which includes consideration of four main aspects:

- Promoting and upholding fairness. AI use should prioritise promoting and upholding fairness, ensuring that all individuals, communities and groups are provided greater opportunities and are not unfairly discriminated against-especially those from diverse, disadvantaged, geographically varied and marginalised backgrounds and statuses. This includes respecting cultural rights and creating opportunities for personalised learning tailored to individual needs. AI use should present diverse viewpoints and prevent reinforcements of bias.
- Equitable access . To achieve equitable access to AI systems, educational institutions should provide affordable, high-quality internet connectivity, devices, digital skills, and high-quality digital learning resources and tools. These should be available both in and out of school to learners and educators, with a consideration for regional, rural and remote communities and students with disabilities. To do this, robust physical and digital infrastructures will be required. Blanket AI bans should be avoided to prevent increasing the digital divide.
- Designing AI tools. In designing AI tools, reducing biases, such as gender bias, is critical to prevent long-term negative effects on users. This involves using diverse, non-biased, representative training data and adopting inclusive design approaches that reflect various perspectives. Designers should understand the impact their design will have.
- Evaluation. When evaluating AI systems, their impact on vulnerable and marginalised groups should be explicitly included, as well as the measurement of diversity, inclusion, and equality, with educators playing an active role in evaluating AI's accuracy, cultural awareness, and inclusiveness to ensure all students are supported equitably. Safeguards and oversight are also essential to mitigate potential risks associated with AI use in educational settings.

## Human, child, and worker rights

When integrating AI into teaching and learning, strive to respect and promote the rights of humans, children, and workers. In enacting this principle, it is vital to consider relevant legislation and regulations.

When considering human rights, for example, it is important to address the right to privacy and recognise the risks of algorithmic bias, which could result in discriminatory outcomes based on factors such as gender, age, or race. Similarly, with children's rights, it is important to ensure they have access to remedies when their rights are violated and allow them to freely express their views on matters that affect them. Regarding the rights of teachers and workers, for example, AI should not be used to exploit educators, undermine their professional autonomy, or increase their workload without adequate support. Practical examples of AI systems for teaching and learning A diverse range of AI systems are available to enhance teaching and learning, each offering distinct functionalities and benefits. This section is divided into two parts: the first explores education-specific AI systems designed to address particular pedagogical needs, while the second examines the potential applications of generative AI systems, such as ChatGPT, for teaching and learning.

## Education-specific AI systems

There is a diverse range of ways of using AI to promote teaching and learning. In terms of existing applications or programs, these can be grouped into four primary categories, excluding generative AI and applications focused on advancing wellbeing and social interaction (see Wang et al., 2024, for a review):

1. Adaptive learning and personalised tutoring. Systems designed to personalise the learning process and create an adaptive environment tailored to learner's knowledge, emotional, or interest states.
2. Intelligent assessment and management . Systems designed for tracking and recording students' learning progress and providing timely evaluation by automating grading, supporting collaborative learning, and managing educational resources
3. Profiling and prediction . Systems that analyse educational data to identify learner characteristics, predict academic outcomes, foster student autonomy, and help educators identify and support at-risk students.
4. Emerging technologies or products . Systems involving emerging technologies and products, such as educational robots, virtual reality (VR), and augmented reality (AR) applications.

Each category can be further broken into further systems (see Figure 3).

Generative AI tools like ChatGPT are often highlighted in discussions on teaching and learning due to their accessibility and low cost. However, other specialised AI systems, such as those indicated in Figure 3, should not be overlooked. While these systems may require more resources, they can offer unique advantages that complement more readily available tools.

Firstly, these specialised AI applications may often deliver deeper integration with curriculum needs, institutional data, and targeted support for specific educational contexts than current generative AI systems. Unlike general tools, they can be tailored to meet the nuanced requirements of various subjects or learner groups, enhancing the quality of instruction and potentially leading to better educational outcomes.

Secondly, while these systems may have higher upfront costs, their long-term benefits could offset the initial investment. By automating administrative tasks, enabling timelier and more targeted interventions, and fostering of engagement through advanced analytics, these tools can contribute to efficiency gains and provide value over time.

Additionally, these tools can address educational equity by supporting a range of learning needs that may go unaddressed by more generalised AI systems. Specialised AI applications can, for example, offer enhanced support for students with disability or language barriers, bridging gaps that free tools alone may not fill.

Another key consideration is data privacy and security. Many free generative AI tools come with privacy concerns related to data handling, which can be problematic in educational environments. The systems in Figure 3 may have been designed with stronger privacy protections and compliance features, making it more likely that they align with institutional standards for student data security.

Finally, these specialised tools can complement free AI tools rather than replace them. Using advanced systems from Figure 3 in tandem with generative AI can create a balanced, effective educational ecosystem where each tool serves its purpose optimally, supporting both the immediate needs of educators and the broader goals of educational institutions.

It is important to note that the synthesis by Wang et al. (2024) did not include AI systems explicitly designed to enhance wellbeing and social interaction. However, as highlighted in Principle 2, which emphasises the advancement of wellbeing and social interaction, educators should also consider the potential of such AI systems. For instance, AI-driven chatbots like Tess (X2AI.com) and Woebot (Woebothealth.com), which are specifically designed to support mental health, have demonstrated benefits in promoting wellbeing, including reductions in depression and anxiety (e.g. Fitzpatrick et al., 2017).

## Generative AI

In addition to education-specific AI systems, such as bespoke adaptive learning platforms, generative AI systems offer significant potential for enhancing teaching and learning. Tools like ChatGPT can perform various functions, including generating text, images, videos, voice recordings, presentations, and podcasts. As such, trying to enumerate all possible use cases for generative AI for teaching and learning would be overwhelming. When using generative AI, it is important to note that it can have numerous limitations, such as:

- inaccurate and unreliable outputs;
- limitations in simulating critical and problem-solving thinking;
- ethical, legal, and privacy concerns, such as issues with academic integrity, distinguishing human-generated versus machine-generated content, copyright infringement, and potential bias in responses;
- potential adverse effects on users' learning and development, including issues with overreliance, risks of bias, knowledge limitations, reliance on biased datasets, and weakening of personal agency and responsibility; and
- technical constraints in input and output, such as difficulties with using technical signs and symbols, prompting, question complexity, and word limits (Cong-Lem et al., 2024).
